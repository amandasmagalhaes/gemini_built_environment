Comparison between virtual audits and Gemini

Published

June 8, 2026

1 Aim

To compare built environment characteristics collected through virtual audit and automated assessment using Gemini in a subsample of street segments from Belo Horizonte.

2 Virtual audit

The ELSI-Urbe Virtual Audit subproject was conducted using a systematic observation method between April and October 2024, based on available Google Street View images. The audit was performed at the street-segment level, considering both sides of the street, and later aggregated into census tracts, which were defined as neighborhoods. The sampling process was carried out in stages, sequentially selecting municipalities (the most populous municipality participating in ELSI-Brazil from each federative unit, with 20 available), census tracts (with ELSI-Brazil interviews), and random street segments. Data collection was conducted by a single trained rater using a multidimensional instrument and a field manual.

In the present analysis, we included only street segments from Belo Horizonte with at least five audited built environment characteristics available (n = 94 segments), namely: sidewalks, crosswalks, speed bumps, potholes, traffic lights, stop signs, sidewalk obstructions, bike lanes, trees, medians, lane markings, crossing signs, bus lanes, bus stops, and roundabouts.

3 Gemini

The same street segments and image dates included in the virtual audit were automatically processed using Google Street View images and a multimodal large language model (Gemini, Google) through a fully automated pipeline. For each coordinate, eight static images covering the full 360° view were selected within a 10-meter radius. These images were downloaded, preprocessed, and analyzed.

A prompt-based approach was used to classify built environment characteristics under two classification schemes (binary and categorical) and with two multimodal large language models (Gemini 2.5 Flash and Gemini 2.5 Pro).

In addition to the characteristics comparable to the virtual audit, the automated approach also evaluated a broader set of characteristics, including: curbs, streetlights, parked vehicles, yield signs, pedestrian signals, kiosks, bollards, median barriers, traffic signs, school zones, parking lanes, and BRT stations.

However, for the present analyses, only characteristics comparable to the virtual audit were considered, resulting in a final sample of 60 street segments with complete data from both approaches.

4 Statistical analysis

Descriptive frequencies were calculated for each characteristic in both approaches. Agreement between the virtual audit and the automated assessment was evaluated using observed agreement (%) and Cohen’s kappa coefficient, with corresponding 95% confidence intervals.

Kappa values were not estimated for characteristics with insufficient variability (i.e., when one or both approaches classified all observations into a single category), and were therefore reported as not applicable (NA).

The automated assessment using Gemini was implemented in Python. Data management and dataset organization were conducted in Stata, and all statistical analyses were performed in R.

5 Results

Agreement between the audit and the Gemini models ranged from slight to substantial. Gemini 2.5 Flash generally outperformed Gemini 2.5 Pro, yielding higher kappa values for most variables with estimable agreement. Substantial agreement was observed for median, traffic light, and bus stop in both models, although kappa values were consistently higher for Gemini 2.5 Flash. Moderate agreement was found for poor street condition (pothole), while fair agreement was observed for crosswalk, speed bumps, stop sign, lane markings, pedestrian crossing sign, and roundabout. Sidewalk obstruction showed slight agreement in Gemini 2.5 Flash and non-estimable kappa in Gemini 2.5 Pro.

Kappa could not be estimated for sidewalk, bike lane, bus lane, and trees because of insufficient variability in ratings. Although observed agreement was generally high (>80% for most variables), kappa values were substantially lower for several characteristics, indicating that part of the agreement was attributable to chance and to the highly imbalanced prevalence of some features.

# Kappa with CI
calc_kappa <- function(data, var) {
  x <- data[[paste0(var, "_0")]]
  y <- data[[paste0(var, "_1")]]
  valid <- !is.na(x) & !is.na(y)
  x <- x[valid]
  y <- y[valid]
  if (length(unique(x)) < 2 | length(unique(y)) < 2) {
    return(c(kappa = NA, lwr = NA, upr = NA))
  }
  k <- psych::cohen.kappa(cbind(x, y))
  ci <- k$confid[1, ]
  c(
    kappa = unname(ci["estimate"]),
    lwr = unname(ci["lower"]),
    upr = unname(ci["upper"])
  )
}

5.3 Diagnostic performance

Sensitivity and specificity, which reflect the ability to correctly identify the presence or absence of a characteristic using the field audit as the reference, were generally high for more visually distinctive features, including trees, sidewalks, traffic lights, roundabouts, medians, and bus stops. Both Gemini models achieved perfect or near-perfect sensitivity for sidewalks, trees, medians, and roundabouts, while specificity was particularly high for trees, traffic lights, speed bumps, and roundabouts. In contrast, lower sensitivity and/or specificity values were observed for speed bumps, stop signs, crosswalks, lane markings, and poor street condition (pothole), indicating greater difficulty in detecting or distinguishing these features consistently.

Positive predictive value (PPV), which indicates the probability that a feature is truly present when classified as present, was highest for trees and sidewalks (PPV = 1.00), followed by lane markings, potholes, and bus stops. Lower PPV values were observed for roundabouts, crosswalks, pedestrian crossing signs, and traffic lights, suggesting a higher proportion of false-positive classifications for these characteristics. Negative predictive value (NPV), which indicates the probability that a feature is truly absent when classified as absent, was generally high across most variables, reaching 1.00 for trees, sidewalks, medians, and roundabouts. However, NPV was substantially lower for lane markings, stop signs, potholes, and sidewalk obstructions, particularly for Gemini 2.5 Pro.

Similarly, F1-scores, which summarize the balance between sensitivity and PPV, were highest for trees and sidewalks (F1 = 1.00), followed by lane markings, potholes, medians, bus stops, traffic lights, and sidewalk obstructions. Lower F1-scores were observed for crosswalks, pedestrian crossing signs, stop signs, speed bumps, and roundabouts, reflecting the challenges in achieving both high detection rates and high classification precision for these features. Overall, performance was broadly similar between Gemini 2.5 Flash and Gemini 2.5 Pro, with Gemini 2.5 Flash showing slightly higher F1-scores for several variables, including medians, traffic lights, lane markings, bus stops, and stop signs.

Diagnostic performance data preparation 
# Function to calculate all metrics
calc_metrics <- function(data, var) {
  ref <- data[[paste0(var, "_0")]]
  test <- data[[paste0(var, "_1")]]
  valid <- !is.na(ref) & !is.na(test)
  ref <- ref[valid]
  test <- test[valid]
  TP <- sum(ref == 1 & test == 1)
  TN <- sum(ref == 0 & test == 0)
  FP <- sum(ref == 0 & test == 1)
  FN <- sum(ref == 1 & test == 0)
  tibble(
    variable = var_labels[var],
    sensitivity = ifelse((TP + FN) == 0, NA, TP / (TP + FN)),
    specificity = ifelse((TN + FP) == 0, NA, TN / (TN + FP)),
    ppv = ifelse((TP + FP) == 0, NA, TP / (TP + FP)),
    npv = ifelse((TN + FN) == 0, NA, TN / (TN + FN)),
    f1_score = ifelse(
      (2 * TP + FP + FN) == 0,
      NA,
      2 * TP / (2 * TP + FP + FN)
    )
  )
}

5.4 ROC

Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) analyses, which evaluate discriminatory ability by comparing sensitivity with the false positive rate, generally supported the findings from the agreement and classification metrics. The highest discriminatory performance was observed for roundabouts, medians, traffic lights, bus stops, and pedestrian crossing signs. Crosswalks, lane markings, potholes, and stop signs showed moderate discrimination, while speed bumps and particularly sidewalk obstruction exhibited the weakest performance. Differences between Gemini 2.5 Flash and Gemini 2.5 Pro were generally small; Gemini 2.5 Flash showed slightly better discrimination for crosswalks, lane markings, stop signs, and potholes, whereas Gemini 2.5 Pro performed marginally better for speed bumps.

# Function to calculate ROC object + AUC
calc_roc_data <- function(data, var, model_name) {
  ref <- data[[paste0(var, "_0")]]
  pred <- data[[paste0(var, "_1")]]
  valid <- !is.na(ref) & !is.na(pred)
  ref <- ref[valid]
  pred <- pred[valid]
  if (length(unique(ref)) < 2) {
    return(NULL)
  }
  roc_obj <- roc(
    ref,
    pred,
    quiet = TRUE
  )
  tibble(
    specificity = roc_obj$specificities,
    sensitivity = roc_obj$sensitivities
  ) %>%
    mutate(
      fpr = 1 - specificity,
      variable = var_labels[var],
      auc = as.numeric(auc(roc_obj)),
      model = model_name
    )
}

Overall, across agreement, classification, and discrimination metrics, Gemini 2.5 Flash generally performed slightly better than Gemini 2.5 Pro, although differences were modest. Both models achieved their best results for larger, permanent, and visually distinctive urban features, such as bus stops, medians, traffic lights, trees, and roundabouts. These findings suggest that multimodal artificial intelligence may be a useful tool for automated urban audits of clearly visible built-environment characteristics, although caution is warranted when evaluating more ambiguous, context-dependent, or less visually salient street features.

6 Discrepancy analysis

In this step, discrepancies between human and AI assessments are examined for features selected due to their intermediate and lower performance in agreement and diagnostic metrics. For each variable, segments with disagreement are identified, the type of discrepancy is classified (human = 1 and AI = 0; or human = 0 and AI = 1), and these cases are linked to geographic coordinates and Google Street View links, enabling visual inspection and interpretation of the differences.

# Function: capture both discrepancy types
get_ids_discrepancy <- function(data, base_var) {

  var0 <- paste0(base_var, "_0")  # human
  var1 <- paste0(base_var, "_1")  # AI

  data %>%
    dplyr::filter(
      !is.na(.data[[var0]]),
      !is.na(.data[[var1]]),
      .data[[var0]] != .data[[var1]]
    ) %>%
